RECENCY WINDOW — INCLUDE ANYTHING FROM THE LAST 7 DAYS:
- The window is the last 7 days (one week) for ALL item types — news, papers, bills, court
  filings, regulatory actions, and reports alike. Favor the newest within that week.
- FIRST, COMPUTE THE CUTOFF DATE: subtract 7 days from today's date (given at the top of this
  message) and write down that exact YYYY-MM-DD cutoff before you start gathering entries.
  Every item's date must be on or after that cutoff. Do this arithmetic explicitly — don't
  eyeball it — this is exactly the kind of off-by-a-few-days error that's easy to make silently.
- PIN DOWN A DATE FOR EVERY ITEM. For each entry, find the item's actual publication /
  filing / release date in the search results and record it in the entry's "date" field
  (YYYY-MM-DD). This is not optional bookkeeping — forcing yourself to locate a real date is
  the main defense against stale results. If, after genuinely looking, you cannot find any
  date for an item, leave "date" empty rather than guessing.
- Check each item's date against the cutoff you computed. If you can affirmatively date it as
  OLDER than the cutoff, DROP it — search engines (and trackers) routinely surface weeks- or
  months-old pages, so don't be fooled by a stale page ranking highly or a tracker listing it
  prominently. Be strict: a polished, relevant-looking page is not in-window unless its date is.
- FINAL PASS BEFORE YOU OUTPUT: once you have a draft list of entries, go back through every
  single one and re-check its "date" against the cutoff. Drop anything that fails, even if it
  came from a trusted tracker or feels important. Do this as a distinct last step, not just
  once while gathering — this catches items that slipped through under time pressure earlier.
- TIE-BREAKER — WHEN IN DOUBT, INCLUDE: if an item is clearly topical and looks recent but
  you cannot pin down an exact date, keep it rather than dropping it. Only drop items you can
  positively confirm are old. Do NOT discard good, on-topic material just because the date is
  fuzzy.
- For a genuinely new development about an older, still-developing story (a ruling in an
  existing case, a new paper version, a bill advancing a stage, a ban entering a new day),
  include it — but make the title and one-sentence summary about WHAT IS NEW this week, not a
  recap of the original event.

================================================================================
ACCURACY — DO NOT HALLUCINATE (READ THIS)
================================================================================
A confident wrong detail is worse than a vague correct one. Web search returns
short snippets, not full pages, and the temptation is to "fill in" specifics from
memory or inference — especially for items you think you already recognize. DO NOT.
Everything in a title or summary must come from text you ACTUALLY SAW in the search
results for THAT item.

- Do not guess. If you did not see a fact in the search results, do not state it.
  Write a more general summary instead of inventing the missing specifics.
- Use the source's OWN wording for titles and key facts. Prefer quoting or closely
  paraphrasing what the page actually says over a slicker paraphrase that adds
  detail the source didn't give you.
- Watch out for items you think you already know (a paper, a company, an ongoing
  case). Recognizing something is exactly when hallucination is most likely — verify
  every name, number, date, venue, and quote against the search results anyway.
- When unsure whether a detail is real, leave it out. A vaguer but correct sentence
  always beats a specific but possibly false one.

WHAT MAKES A GOOD DIGEST
================================================================================
- If several articles cover the same development, group them into ONE entry — but include
  only the 2-3 BEST links (prefer the primary source plus at most one or two strong
  secondary articles). Do not dump every article you found; more than 3 links per entry is
  redundant and clutters the digest.
- Each entry gets a short, specific title and a ONE-sentence description (plain,
  concrete, no hype — say what happened and why it matters).
- Each entry is labeled with exactly one tag — Paper, Policy, Lawsuit, News, or Misc —
  matching the four content buckets below (use Misc for anything that fits none).
- Prioritize genuinely new and substantive items: new papers, bills, regulatory
  actions, court filings, official reports, major incidents, notable launches. Skip pure
  marketing, rumor, and low-signal social media chatter.
- Prefer primary sources (the paper, the bill text, the official statement, the
  court filing) and add a secondary news link only when it adds context.
- AIM FOR 10+ ENTRIES. The 7-day window almost always contains at least this many
  relevant items. If you find yourself about to return fewer than ~6 entries, STOP: that
  usually means you under-searched, not that the week was quiet. Go back and run more
  searches (the broad generic queries especially) and re-check each content category before
  finalizing. Don't pad with off-topic filler — but a 2-3 entry digest is a red flag that
  you missed things, not a sign of a slow week.

SEARCH STRATEGY — COVER ALL FOUR CONTENT TYPES, NOT JUST NEWS
================================================================================
General web search surfaces news articles much more readily than papers or bills, so
you MUST run dedicated searches for each content type below every time. Do not let news
crowd out papers and policy. Aim for a mix across these buckets:

1. ACADEMIC PAPERS — recent arXiv work on the topics above (jailbreaks/red-teaming,
   evaluations/audits, robustness, open-weight model safety, frontier risks); check arXiv
   listing/search pages directly. As a starting point for surfacing recent on-topic papers,
   use the redirect link https://vjunetxuuftofi.github.io/arxivredirect/ — it points to the
   full list of CS papers submitted to arXiv the prior day (usually a few hundred), which you
   should scan for on-topic work — and follow through to the underlying arXiv papers. Aim for
   2-4 paper entries when any exist in the window.

2. BILLS, LEGISLATION & REGULATION — new AI bills, executive actions, regulatory filings,
   and official guidance from the US (Congress, states, agencies, CAISI), EU (AI Act,
   Commission), UK (AISI, government), and China. Include substantive items in the window.
   START by fetching the AI Governance Institute tracker (see TRACKERS below) — it is
   updated daily and is the most reliable way to catch regulatory actions that general web
   search buries under news. Then run your own searches to fill gaps.

3. LAWSUITS & COURT FILINGS — new complaints, rulings, and settlements in cases involving
   AI or AI companies, in priority order: (a) tort suits (product liability, negligence,
   wrongful death — e.g. chatbot/AI-product harms), (b) other suits against frontier
   developers (copyright/IP, privacy, consumer protection, securities), (c) government
   enforcement (FTC, state AGs, DOJ). START by fetching the two litigation trackers listed
   under TRACKERS below — general web search is unreliable for new filings, and these are
   maintained case-by-case with dates. Then also check legal outlets (Reuters Legal, Law360,
   Bloomberg Law) and court-document sources (CourtListener/RECAP), and link the actual
   complaint or ruling as the primary source. Favor new filings over routine procedural
   updates.
